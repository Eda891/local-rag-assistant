# Wilderness Survival — Reference Answers

## Forest Survival

**Summary**: Prioritize shelter and water over fire and food; insulate yourself from the ground first.

**Safety Warnings**
- Avoid dead trees/branches ("widowmakers") when choosing a shelter site.
- Treat all natural water before drinking, even fast-moving streams.
- Wet clothing causes heat loss much faster than people expect, even in mild temperatures.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Choose slightly elevated, dry ground away from dead branches.
2. Insulate the ground beneath you with leaves or boughs before worrying about a roof.
3. Build a simple lean-to from a fallen branch and leaf litter for wind/rain cover.
4. Follow terrain downhill or animal trails to find water; boil for at least one minute before drinking.
5. Gather tinder, kindling, and fuel wood before lighting a fire.
6. If lost, stay near your last known point rather than wandering; use three of any signal (fires, whistle blasts) for rescue.

## Mountain Survival

**Summary**: Get below the treeline and out of the wind fast — mountain weather and altitude illness can turn dangerous within an hour.

**Safety Warnings**
- Avoid exposed ridgelines/summits during storms due to lightning risk.
- Descending is often more dangerous than ascending due to fatigue.
- Confusion or coughing up fluid at altitude signals a medical emergency requiring immediate descent.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Move below the treeline or behind a windbreak as soon as weather turns.
2. Turn back early if clouds are building — mountain weather changes fast.
3. Watch for headache, nausea, and dizziness as early signs of altitude sickness.
4. If symptoms worsen, descend to lower elevation immediately — this is the only reliable treatment.
5. Melt snow for water rather than eating it directly.
6. Use bright gear or an SOS light/mirror pattern (3 short, 3 long, 3 short) to signal rescuers.

**Reference**: mountain_survival.txt — "Weather and Terrain" / "Altitude Illness"

## Snow and Extreme Cold Survival

**Summary**: Staying dry is as critical as staying warm; a small, well-insulated shelter beats a large one.

**Safety Warnings**
- Never eat snow directly — it lowers core body temperature.
- Ventilate any enclosed snow shelter to avoid carbon dioxide buildup.
- Do not rub frostbitten skin; rewarm gradually, not with direct high heat.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Dress in layers: moisture-wicking base, insulating mid-layer, wind/waterproof outer layer.
2. Build a small snow shelter (cave or quinzee) sized just for your body, with a ventilation hole.
3. Insulate the ground beneath you before worrying about warmth from above.
4. Melt snow for drinking water rather than eating it directly.
5. Watch for hypothermia (shivering, confusion) and frostbite (numb, waxy skin) in yourself and others.
6. Signal with stomped symbols (X or SOS) in open snowfields, visible from the air.

**Reference**: snow_survival.txt — "Shelter" / "Recognizing Cold Injuries"

## Rainy and Wet-Climate Survival

**Summary**: Wet skin plus wind causes hypothermia fast even in mild temperatures — prioritize drying out and steep-angled shelter roofs.

**Safety Warnings**
- Wet cotton loses nearly all insulating value; synthetic/wool retains more.
- Standing water can still carry contamination even in a rain-soaked environment.
- Avoid camping in dry creek beds or gullies due to flash flood risk.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Build a steep-angled shelter roof (leaves, bark, tarp) so water sheds rather than pools.
2. Raise your sleeping area off wet ground for insulation.
3. Wring out and dry clothing whenever possible, even partially.
4. Collect rainwater directly before it touches the ground; treat before drinking regardless.
5. Dry your feet and change socks regularly to prevent skin breakdown.
6. Mark your route in fog/heavy rain to avoid getting turned around.

**Reference**: rain_wet_survival.txt — "Shelter" / "Staying Warm When Wet"

## Desert Survival

**Summary**: Ration sweat, not water — reduce activity and seek shade rather than restricting how much you drink.

**Safety Warnings**
- Heatstroke (hot dry skin, confusion) is a medical emergency requiring immediate cooling.
- Deserts can drop near freezing at night despite scorching days.
- Avoid dry washes/low areas — flash floods can occur even without visible rain nearby.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Seek shade and limit activity during peak heat hours; move in early morning or evening instead.
2. Drink according to need — don't ration water below what your body requires.
3. Look for water at cliff bases, damp dry riverbeds, or where vegetation/animal tracks concentrate.
4. Cover skin with light, loose clothing to reduce water loss from sweat evaporation.
5. Watch for heat exhaustion (heavy sweating, weakness) and treat with shade and rehydration before it progresses.
6. Keep warm layers accessible for cold desert nights.

## General Survival Skills

**Summary**: The "Rule of Threes" (air, shelter, water, food) helps prioritize actions across any environment.

**Safety Warnings**
- Don't self-ration water below your body's needs — dehydration impairs judgment.
- Always build fires in a controllable way, away from dry vegetation.
- Panic increases the chance of poor decisions; pause and assess before acting.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Prioritize shelter and exposure protection first, then water, then food.
2. Stay put if lost and your route is known to others; leave markers if you must move.
3. Insulate yourself from the ground before worrying about the shelter roof.
4. Prepare all fire materials (tinder, kindling, fuel) before attempting to light it.
5. Use the number three for distress signals — fires, whistle blasts, flashes.
6. Treat injuries promptly and conserve energy and water for what's ahead.

**Reference**: general_survival_skills.txt — "The Rule of Threes" / "Signaling for Rescue"
