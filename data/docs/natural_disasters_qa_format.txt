# Natural Disasters — Reference Answers

## Earthquake

**Summary**: Drop, cover, and hold on during shaking; most injuries come from falling objects and panicked movement, not the ground itself.

**Safety Warnings**
- Do not run outside during shaking.
- Watch for gas leaks and aftershocks afterward.
- Near a coast, move to high ground immediately if shaking was strong or prolonged — don't wait for an official tsunami warning.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Drop to your hands and knees immediately.
2. Cover your head and neck with your arms; crawl under sturdy furniture if possible.
3. Hold on until shaking stops completely.
4. If driving, pull over away from bridges/overpasses and stay in the vehicle.
5. After shaking stops, check for injuries and gas leaks before re-entering any structure.
6. Expect aftershocks and stay alert for hours to days afterward.

## Flood

**Summary**: Get to higher ground early and never enter moving floodwater — it's more dangerous than it looks.

**Safety Warnings**
- Six inches of fast-moving water can knock down an adult; one to two feet can sweep away a vehicle.
- Floodwater may be contaminated or electrically charged.
- Mold can begin growing within 24–48 hours after water exposure.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Move to higher ground as soon as heavy rain or flood warnings are issued.
2. Never walk, swim, or drive through floodwater ("Turn Around, Don't Drown").
3. If your vehicle is caught in rising water, abandon it for higher ground.
4. If trapped indoors, move to the highest level (avoid sealed attics).
5. After the flood, wait for official "safe to return" confirmation.
6. Discard food that contacted floodwater and watch for mold during cleanup.

**Reference**: flood.txt — "During a Flood" / "After a Flood"

## Hurricane

**Summary**: Evacuate early if ordered, then shelter in an interior room away from windows if riding out the storm.

**Safety Warnings**
- The calm "eye" is not the end of the storm — winds return violently from the opposite direction.
- Never run a generator indoors or near windows/doors (carbon monoxide risk).
- Treat all downed power lines as live.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Evacuate promptly if ordered — don't wait for conditions to worsen.
2. If sheltering in place, move to an interior room on the lowest safe floor, away from glass.
3. Stay inside during the eye of the storm; wind returns from the opposite direction.
4. Keep a battery-powered radio on for updates once power fails.
5. After the storm, avoid floodwater and downed lines, and run generators outdoors only.
6. Boil or use bottled water until officials confirm safety.

**Reference**: hurricane.txt — "During a Hurricane" / "After a Hurricane"

## Wildfire

**Summary**: Evacuate immediately when told to — fire can outrun both people and vehicles — and prepare your home before leaving if time allows.

**Safety Warnings**
- Do not attempt to defend a property once evacuation is mandatory.
- Smoke inhalation, not flame contact, causes most wildfire deaths.
- Do not return home until authorities confirm it's safe — hot spots can reignite.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Evacuate immediately upon official notice; don't wait to assess the fire yourself.
2. Close all windows/doors before leaving; leave interior doors unlocked for firefighters.
3. If trapped in a vehicle, stay inside, close vents, and duck below window level.
4. If caught outdoors, seek already-burned ground or bare earth and lie face down.
5. After the fire, wear an N95 mask for ash/smoke and watch for hot spots and unstable trees.
6. Avoid tap water until officials confirm safety.

**Reference**: wildfire.txt — "During a Wildfire" / "After a Wildfire"

## Tornado

**Summary**: Take shelter in the lowest, most interior room immediately upon a tornado warning — don't wait to see the funnel.

**Safety Warnings**
- Do not shelter under an overpass; wind speed can increase there.
- A "watch" means conditions are favorable; a "warning" means act now.
- Avoid unnecessary movement if trapped in debris — tap or whistle rather than shout to conserve energy and be heard.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Go to your safe room (interior, windowless, lowest floor) the moment a warning is issued.
2. Get low, cover your head and neck, and use a mattress or heavy furniture for cover.
3. If driving, seek a sturdy building, or as a last resort get low in a ditch away from the vehicle.
4. If caught outdoors, lie flat in the lowest available spot.
5. After the tornado, watch for downed lines, gas leaks, and unstable structures before moving through the area.
6. If trapped, signal with a tapping sound or whistle rather than shouting.

**Reference**: tornado.txt — "During a Tornado" / "After a Tornado"

## Winter Storm / Extreme Cold

**Summary**: Stay indoors, dress in layers, and never run fuel-burning equipment indoors — carbon monoxide is a leading cause of post-storm deaths.

**Safety Warnings**
- Never use a generator, camp stove, or grill indoors or in a garage.
- Watch for hypothermia (confusion, slurred speech) and frostbite (numb, waxy skin).
- Avoid overexertion (e.g., snow shoveling) if you have a heart condition.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Stay indoors; if you must go out, wear layered clothing covering exposed skin.
2. Watch yourself and others for signs of hypothermia or frostbite.
3. Never run generators or fuel-burning equipment indoors.
4. If stranded in a vehicle, stay inside, run the engine periodically for heat, and signal with a bright cloth.
5. After the storm, check pipes for freezing before restoring water flow, and watch for ice on walkways.
6. Discard food from an extended power outage per food-safety guidance.

**Reference**: winter_storm.txt — "During a Winter Storm" / "After a Winter Storm"

## Tsunami

**Summary**: Strong coastal shaking or a sudden ocean recession means move inland or to high ground immediately — don't wait for an official warning.

**Safety Warnings**
- If you can see the wave, you are likely too close to escape it.
- Multiple waves can arrive over hours; the first is often not the largest.
- Stay away from rivers/streams leading to the ocean — tsunamis can travel upstream.

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. After strong coastal shaking, move to high ground or inland immediately once shaking stops.
2. If the ocean recedes rapidly or you hear a loud roar, evacuate without waiting for confirmation.
3. Move on foot if roads are likely to be jammed or damaged.
4. If high ground is unreachable, go to an upper floor of a sturdy building.
5. After the first wave, stay away from the coast until officials confirm all danger has passed.
6. Check for gas leaks and structural damage before re-entering buildings.

**Reference**: tsunami.txt — "During a Tsunami" / "After a Tsunami"

## General Preparedness

**Summary**: A stocked emergency kit and a practiced family communication plan apply across almost any disaster type.

**Safety Warnings**
- Card readers and ATMs may not work without power — keep cash on hand.
- Local phone lines often jam after a disaster; use text messages instead of calls.
- Update kits annually and after major life changes (new baby, medication, pet).

**Step-by-step Guidance**
1. Build a kit with water (1 gal/person/day, 3+ days), non-perishable food, first aid, flashlight, radio, and cash.
2. Store copies of key documents in a waterproof container.
3. Choose an out-of-town contact and two meeting places (near-home and outside the neighborhood).
4. Practice the plan with household drills at least yearly.
5. During evacuation, take your kit, medications, and documents, and notify your out-of-town contact.
6. After any disaster, check for injuries first, then document damage before cleanup.

**Reference**: general_preparedness.txt — "Building a Basic Emergency Kit" / "Making a Family Communication Plan"
